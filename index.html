Abstract

 
This paper explores dark matter and its effect on gravitational lensing, highlighting key discoveries and applications.
The 30.1% of dark matter suggested to be in our universe was first hypothesised by Fritz Zwicky but their theory was
later confirmed by Vern Rubin in the 1970s as she observed the velocities of stars and how their motion did not match
the quantity of visible matter. Gravitational lensing occurs when light is deflected due to the mass of a large celestial 
object, for example, a star. There are three types of gravitational lensing - strong, weak and microlensing, which could
be used to produce large-scale maps of the universe's structure.

Discovery and Proof of Dark Matter:

In 1933, Fritz Zwicky studied the ‚ÄôComa Cluster‚Äô which is made up of upwards of thousands of galaxies, approx 330 million light years away.
During his studies, Zwicky observed that the galaxies were moving too fast in a cluster compared to the visible mass thus concluding that
there was not enough mass to hold the cluster together. To determine why there is a missing mass, Zwicky measured the predictable brightness
of a known star, an observation known as the standard candle and compared that value to the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram (see Figure 1.)


      
Since light obeys the inverse square law, Zwicky was able to estimate the mass of the stars in the galaxy based on the overall brightness 
of the galaxy. However, to figure out the speed the observed galaxy was moving at, Fritz Zwicky needed to apply the ‚ÄòDoppler Redshift Effect‚Äô- 
the Doppler effect describes how the relative motion of the source and the sensor alters the frequency of electromagnetic or sound waves
(Jonathan D. Kaunitz, 2016) - to calculate the change in wavelength of light emitted by the galaxy. Since the clusters move freely under gravity, 
Fritz Zwicky applied the Virial Theorem which results in this equation, m=v2rG. However, Zwicky‚Äôs calculated mass was not equal to the mass of
visible matter, concluding that there must be invisible matter (dark matter). Almost 40 years later, Vera Rubin was researching the motion of

Uses of Gravitational Lensing in Astronomy
Arguably, weak gravitational lensing could be used to map out the outskirts of large galaxy clusters and trace the large-scale structures due to
their effect on other galaxies. Currently, this method faces the challenge of being unable to differentiate between the different sources of redshifts.
However, once this challenge is overcome, it will be possible to produce a three-dimensional map of matter in the universe rather than the current light 
map. Thus providing the most understating of structure formation and evolution alongside determining the content of the universe matter. Alternatively,
the basic derivations of the gravitational lens equation done by Sjur Refsdal showed how the gravitational lens effect could be used to determine Hubble‚Äôs 
constant by measuring the time delay between two lensed images. In addition, another use might be as a strong magnifying lens which would boost the amount 
of studies into the early evolutionary stage, with the earliest possible stage being infant or proto-galaxies relatively short after the big bang.
